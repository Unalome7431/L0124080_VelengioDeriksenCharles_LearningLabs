What's in Pontianak?

Pontianak is the capital city of West Kalimantan, situated on the island of Borneo in Indonesia. Founded in 1771, it initially began as a trading port and eventually became the chief gold city of Borneo. Located near the equator, Pontianak occupies approximately 118 square kilometers in the delta of the Kapuas River, which meets its major tributary, the Landak River. The city is known as "Kota Khatulistiwa" (Equatorial City) because it straddles the Earth's equator.

The population of Pontianak is diverse, with significant communities of Chinese and Malays, alongside indigenous groups such as the Dayaks. The economy is driven by manufacturing, agriculture, and trade, with key industries including shipbuilding, food processing, and rubber production. Cultural richness is evident in local festivals and cuisine, making Pontianak a vibrant hub for both residents and visitors. Notable attractions include the Equator Monument and various natural parks that showcase the region's lush biodiversity.

Tourists visiting Pontianak can explore cultural attractions such as traditional houses like the Rumah Radakng, experience unique local cuisines like spicy porridges and sago noodles, and participate in festivities like the Chinese New Year celebrations. The city is accessible via Supadio Airport, making it a key entry point for travelers heading to West Kalimantan.

Equator Monument

Tugu Khatulistiwa, also known as the Equator Monument, is a significant landmark located in Pontianak, West Kalimantan, Indonesia. This monument marks the spot where the equator passes through the city, making Pontianak one of the few cities in the world situated directly on the equatorial line.

Historical Background

The construction of Tugu Khatulistiwa began in 1928, initiated by a geographic expedition led by a Dutch geographer. The original structure was a simple post marked with an arrow indicating the equatorial line. Over the years, it has undergone several renovations and enhancements:

  • 1930: The first improvement included adding a circular base and an arrow.
  • 1938: Architect Friedrich Silaban redesigned the monument, incorporating four pillars made from belian wood (a type of ironwood), with varying heights.
  • 1990-1991: A protective dome was constructed around the original monument, significantly enlarging its size—five times larger than the original. This new structure was inaugurated on September 21, 1991.

The current monument features two front pillars standing 15.25 meters tall and two rear pillars reaching 22 meters, with a large arrow pointing north-south to signify the equatorial line

Architectural features

Tugu Khatulistiwa consists of:

  • Four belian wood pillars: Two front pillars are 3.05 meters tall, while the rear pillars reach up to 4.4 meters.
  • A circular base: Inscribed with "EVENAAR," which means equator in Dutch, indicating its significance.
  • Directional arrow: The monument includes a prominent arrow that indicates north-south alignment at latitude 0°.

Inside the dome, visitors can find historical photographs and educational displays about astronomy and geography, including information about celestial bodies and their relation to Earth

Cultural Significance

Tugu Khatulistiwa is not just a tourist attraction; it symbolizes Pontianak's unique geographical position. The site is popular among tourists who wish to experience standing on the equator. Notably, during specific times of the year—around March 21 and September 23—the sun is directly overhead at noon, causing objects to cast no shadow at this location

Geographical Features

  • Source: Müller Mountain Range, elevation of 2,278 meters (7,474 feet).
  • Mouth: Discharges into the South China Sea.
  • Basin size: Covers an area of about 98,749 square kilometers (38,127 square miles).
  • Depth: Ranges from 40 to 50 meters (130 to 160 feet) in some areas.

The river flows through diverse terrains, including mountainous regions and lowland forests, forming numerous tributaries and meandering channels. It is navigable for much of its length, serving as a crucial transportation route for local communities.

Cultural and Economic Significance

The Kapuas River plays a vital role in the lives of people in West Kalimantan. It supports agriculture, fishing, and transportation, making it essential for local economies. Historically, it has facilitated trade and cultural exchange among various communities along its banks.

The river is also rich in biodiversity, hosting over 700 species of freshwater fish and various unique ecosystems. Conservation efforts are ongoing to protect these habitats and the species that depend on them.

Tourism

Tourism along the Kapuas River offers opportunities to explore traditional Dayak villages and experience local cultures. Visitors can engage in activities like fishing, boating, and trekking through the surrounding jungles.

Alun-alun Kapuas

Alun-alun Kapuas is a prominent public park located in the heart of Pontianak, West Kalimantan, Indonesia, along the banks of the Kapuas River. This park serves as a recreational area for both locals and visitors, offering stunning views of the river and the surrounding landscape.

Key Features

  • Scenic Views: Alun-alun Kapuas provides picturesque views of the longest river in Indonesia, where visitors can observe boats traversing its waters.
  • Cultural Hub: The park is a popular gathering spot for the community, featuring local culinary offerings and cultural events.
  • Architectural Elements: Among its attractions are beautiful fountains and a replica of the Tugu Khatulistiwa (Equator Monument), symbolizing the city’s geographical significance.
  • Renovations: The park has undergone several renovations since its initial establishment, expanding from 1.5 hectares to approximately 3 hectares, enhancing its facilities and landscaping.

Accessibility

Easily accessible by various modes of transportation, Alun-alun Kapuas is strategically located in the city center, making it a convenient destination for relaxation and leisure activities.

Evening Ambience

At night, the park transforms with illuminated pathways and vibrant lights reflecting off the river, creating a serene atmosphere for evening strolls and social gatherings.

Kadariah Palace

Kadariah Palace, known as Keraton Kadariah, is a historic royal palace located in Pontianak, West Kalimantan, Indonesia. Built between 1771 and 1778, it serves as the seat of the Sultanate of Pontianak and is a significant cultural landmark in the region.

Historical Background

The palace was founded by Sayyid Syarif Abdurrahman Alkadrie, who became the first Sultan of Pontianak after its completion. The establishment of the palace marked the beginning of the Pontianak Sultanate, which played a crucial role in the region's history and development.

Architectural Features

  • Construction: The palace is constructed from high-quality wood, featuring traditional Malay architectural elements.
  • Cannons: It houses 13 ancient cannons made by Portuguese and French artisans, displayed prominently at the front.
  • Interior: Inside, visitors can find historical artifacts, including jewelry, heirlooms, and an antique mirror known as "Kaca Seribu" (Thousand Glass).

Cultural Significance

Kadariah Palace is not only a historical site but also a center for cultural activities. It attracts many visitors who come to learn about the rich heritage of the Sultanate and enjoy its scenic views along the Kapuas River.

Accessibility

Located near the city center, Kadariah Palace is easily accessible by both land and river routes. Visitors can reach it via vehicles or boats from nearby ports.
